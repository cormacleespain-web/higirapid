# Transparency Guide — AI Design Lead

How to make AI behaviour and limitations visible in the product so users can make informed decisions.

## What to Disclose

- **AI involvement**: When a feature uses AI (suggestions, generation, classification), say so in the UI. Use consistent language (e.g. "AI-suggested", "Generated by AI"). Document the wording in AI UX guidelines and microcopy.
- **Data use**: When user input or data is sent to an AI system, disclose it (e.g. in settings, first use, or in-product). Link to privacy policy or data use notice where required.
- **Confidence and uncertainty**: If the product shows confidence scores or "uncertain" states, explain what they mean in user terms (e.g. "Low confidence — please review"). Avoid jargon (e.g. "logprob") in user-facing copy.

## Where to Disclose

- **In-context**: Near the AI output (e.g. label under a suggestion, tooltip on an icon). Prefer persistent for consequential output.
- **Settings / first use**: For data use or opt-in/opt-out, use onboarding or settings. Document in flow and copy guidelines.
- **Help and documentation**: Document how the AI feature works and its limitations in help or knowledge base. UX Tech Writer can own the doc; AI Design Lead provides content and review.

## Guardrails

- Do not bury disclosure in long legal text only; surface key points in the product.
- Do not overclaim (e.g. "AI is always accurate"). Use qualified language (e.g. "AI-assisted; please verify for important decisions").
- Align with AI Engineer on what can be technically disclosed (e.g. model name, data sent) so copy is accurate.
